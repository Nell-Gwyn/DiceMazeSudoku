THE RULES
  1. Normal sudoku rules apply (every row, column, and 3x3 box must contain all the digits 1 to 9 once each).
  2. The finished grid contains a Dice Maze.
    1. The dice starts in r1c1 (the cell labeled "Start"), with a 2 on top and a 1 facing south. The dice must travel from there to r9c8 (the cell labeled "Goal").
    2. The dice moves by rolling orthogonally (use WASD keys, or the labeled buttons on the side panel).
    3. The dice can only move onto a cell that contains the same digit currently on top of the dice.
      • For instance: if a 2 is on top of the dice, the next cell it moves onto must contain a 2. If a 6 is on top after that move, the next cell it moves to from there must contain a 6, and so on.
    4. The dice path can cross the same cell multiple times.
  3. Digits in arrows must sum to the digit in the circle.
    1. If the dice enters an arrow's circle, it must then travel along the entire length of that arrow from the circle to the arrowhead.
      • For instance: if the dice enters r4c6, its next move must be to r3c6, and then r3c7.
  4. Cells marked with a square are even, cells marked with a (solid) circle are odd.
    1. Cells with green shapes are on the dice path, cells with red shapes are not on the dice path. Gray shapes may be either.

For more examples and clarifications, see "About" panel.

(bonus feature: hold down Q and E to, respectively, turn the dice in place without sliding it, or to slide it without turning it, this should help you move it around the board)

(you can also click and drag outside the grid to tilt the maze grid... in case you want to view all sides of the dice without moving it)


dedicated to
ROBERT ABBOTT
(1933 — 2018)

Robert Abbott was a game designer who created several card games and board games, but was perhaps best known for inventing a type of puzzle known as "Logic Mazes," or sometimes "Multi-State Mazes" (or just "mazes-with-rules"). These are, well, mazes with rules.

The first-ever logic maze, Abbott's "The Traffic Maze in Floyd's Knob" (see below) was originally published in 1962, in Martin Gardner's Mathematical Games column in Scientific American. After this, Abbott would go on to publish multiple books of logic mazes, including Mad Mazes and SuperMazes.

A maze from Mad Mazes
(click to expand)

There are many different sub-types of logic maze, some resembling traditional pencil-and-paper mazes just with an added rule regarding movement, others resembling something else entirely.

One sub-type of logic maze that Abbott made a number of were dice mazes, or "Rolling Cube Mazes" as he sometimes called them. The standard form of these involved rolling a dice around a board of numbers, the numbers indicating which cell the dice could travel onto.


a maze from SuperMazes
(click to expand)

These often involved just one number per cell (though often with "wild" squares to shake things up), though there were many other variants on the same premise as well (see below). These grid layouts are what gave me the idea to apply some of these same concepts to a sudoku hybrid.



The solution to the previous maze, and a tutorial on how to set your own (also from SuperMazes).

In fact, he even included a tutorial on how to set your own rolling-cube mazes, a bit reminiscent of the puzzle-setting video series on CTC.

Granted, this tutorial wasn't quite as useful in designing this Dice Maze Sudoku, since the numbers in most of the grid must be deduced, which changes the game considerably (also, half the fun of a logic maze is exploring and finding false paths through trial and error, which is far less fun in a sudoku than in a maze). Still, quite a few of the concepts are still useful (for instance, building the solution path first, as though you were solving a maze that didn't exist yet), and I did my best to replicate the feeling, if not the exact experience, of solving one of these mazes.
